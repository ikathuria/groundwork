# Retraction: Graded bandgap perovskite solar cells

## Summary
Nature Materials retraction (Vol. 17, February 2018, p. 204) of Ergen et al., "Graded bandgap perovskite solar cells", Nature Materials 16, 522–525 (2017; published online 7 November 2016; retracted after print 20 December 2017). The original paper claimed graded-bandgap perovskite/hBN/graphene-aerogel architectures with high photovoltaic performance. The authors retracted because of reproducibility failure: large variability of I-V curves and spectral response among nominally identical devices, and rapid time-evolution of key photovoltaic parameters. This entry is included in the corpus as a Failure-Archaeology cautionary tale: solar-cell efficiency claims under stress (whether perovskite or plated-Cu SHJ) require measurement protocols that demonstrate reproducibility and stability, not just one-shot champion numbers. The lesson is broadly applicable: when a new metallization architecture (e.g. Ni-Cu plated SHJ) reports a single 24%+ champion cell, it must be paired with spread statistics and a stability protocol before the claim can be trusted.

## Key claims
- Original paper (Ergen et al. 2017) claimed solar cells based on two perovskite layers separated by a monolayer of hexagonal boron nitride deposited on graphene aerogel.
- Reasons for retraction: (a) "the large variability of the current-voltage and spectral response of nominally identical devices" and (b) "the rapid time evolution of key photovoltaic parameters" undermined the authors' confidence in the original conclusions.
- All authors agreed with the retraction.
- The retraction does not specify the absolute mechanism of variability or evolution — only that the original confidence interval on architecture performance is no longer warranted.
- Methodological lesson for any new PV architecture / metallization study: champion-cell numbers without a documented reproducibility study (n ≥ 5 cells, full I-V spread, time-stability) and without a documented degradation profile (light soaking, dark anneal, DH) cannot be relied upon for technology-comparison claims.
