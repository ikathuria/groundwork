# Freeze-drying scaffold fabrication

## What it measures / produces

Freeze-drying (lyophilisation) of an aqueous slurry of biopolymer (collagen, collagen-GAG) produces porous scaffolds: the slurry is frozen at a controlled rate, then water is sublimated under vacuum, leaving the scaffold's pore structure templated by the ice crystals. **Pore size is set by the freezing temperature**: lower final temperature → smaller pores. Murphy's group used controlled freezing (–40 to –10 °C final) to produce CG scaffolds with 96–151 μm pores; an annealing step extended the range to 85–325 μm [[../sources/2010-murphy-understanding-the-effect-of-mean-pore-size-on]].

## When to use it

Use freeze-drying for soft polymer/biopolymer scaffolds (collagen, collagen-GAG, gelatin) with controlled mean pore size in the 50–500 μm range. Not applicable to HA ceramic scaffolds (no organic solvent / aqueous slurry of pure ceramic).

## Key parameters

- **Pore-size window**: 85–325 μm achievable in CG scaffolds via freezing-rate + annealing [[../sources/2010-murphy-understanding-the-effect-of-mean-pore-size-on]].
- **Freezing rate**: slow → larger pores; fast → smaller pores.
- **Annealing step**: post-freezing thermal hold to coarsen ice crystals; extends pore-size range upward.

## Things to watch for

- [[../failure-modes/cad-vs-actual-pore-size-mismatch]] — pore size depends on freezing kinetics; verify by SEM.
- [[../failure-modes/edge-effect-in-scaffold-cell-seeding]] — small-pore freeze-dried scaffolds (<150 μm) show pronounced edge-aggregation cell skinning [[../sources/2010-murphy-understanding-the-effect-of-mean-pore-size-on]].
