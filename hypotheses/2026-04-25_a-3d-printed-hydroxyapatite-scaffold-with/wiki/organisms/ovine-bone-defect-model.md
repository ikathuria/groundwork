# Ovine bone defect model

## What it is

Sheep (Merino, Suffolk, etc., 2–4 yr) bone-defect models are the large-animal translational benchmark for orthopedic and spinal-fusion devices. Among large-animal models, sheep have the closest match to adult human long-bone size, bone density, and physiological load magnitudes — making them the preferred model for tibial-segmental, femoral-segmental, and vertebral-interbody-fusion testing. Sheep are increasingly chosen over dog for ethical and animal-welfare reasons in many jurisdictions.

## Why this model for this hypothesis

- Sheep is downstream of the planned in-vitro experiment; included because [[../sources/2013-henkel-bone-regeneration-based-on-tissue-engine]] places it in the standard translational hierarchy.
- Most relevant if the gradient-pore HA scaffold concept is extended to spinal interbody fusion or large-segment long-bone defects, where ovine models are the standard pre-clinical test.

## Typical parameters

- **Strain / age / sex**: Merino or Suffolk, 2–4 yr, female (ewes most common — easier to handle, no testes-related variability).
- **Defect**: tibial / metatarsal 20–30 mm segmental, femoral 25 mm segmental, iliac-crest 15 mm plug, lumbar interbody fusion cage.
- **n**: ≥ 4–6 per group per time point.
- **Time points**: 12, 26, 52 weeks.
- **Readouts**: serial radiographs, μCT, biomechanical testing, histomorphometry, fluorochrome labeling for bone-formation rate.

## Common pitfalls

- [[../failure-modes/brittle-ceramic-mechanical-failure]] — sheep load magnitudes will fracture pure HA scaffolds; mechanical reinforcement or staged loading is required.
- [[../failure-modes/in-vivo-vs-in-vitro-discordance]] — applies; sheep is the closest non-human large-animal model for long-bone work.
- [[../failure-modes/short-term-proliferation-vs-long-term-bone-formation]] — applies at multi-month timescale.
