## Summary

Zhao et al. quantified longitudinal and radial microgradients in cortical thickness, porosity, and Haversian/Volkmann canal diameter along the rabbit femur using high-resolution micro-CT, then correlated these with local compressive moduli. They establish that native bone porosity and canal diameter increase **radially** from the periosteal surface toward the medullary cavity (cortical → cancellous transition), and **longitudinally** from mid-shaft toward the metaphyseal/epiphyseal ends. Specific values: at 250 μm depth porosity is ~5–40% and canal diameter ~40 μm at mid-shaft, rising to ~40% and ~110 μm at the bone ends; at 750 μm depth porosity reaches ~80% and canal diameter ~200 μm at the ends. Compressive modulus drops from ~15 MPa at the dense mid-shaft to ~5 MPa at the porous ends. **This paper is the most direct biomimetic-target reference for the proposed 100→500 μm radial gradient**, providing the structural benchmark a graded scaffold would aim to reproduce.

## Key claims

- Native rabbit cortical bone canal diameters span ~40 μm (mid-shaft, near periosteum) to ~200 μm (bone ends, deeper into cortex) — *the canal-scale gradient observed in vivo is in the 40–200 μm range, smaller than the 100–500 μm range proposed in the hypothesis*.
- Native porosity gradient: ~5% (mid-shaft, periosteal) → ~80% (bone ends, deep) at 750 μm thickness from outer surface.
- Both radial (centripetal) and longitudinal gradients are present and compound: porosity highest at deep medullary regions of metaphyses.
- Compressive modulus: ~15 MPa (mid-shaft) → ~5 MPa (bone ends), decreasing monotonically with porosity and canal diameter.
- Posterior and medial quadrants consistently show higher porosity than anterior and lateral; anatomical anisotropy visible at all radial depths.
- Methodology: rabbit femur sectioned into proximal / mid-shaft / distal regions; high-resolution μCT at "micron-scale resolutions"; mechanical compression on slices for local modulus.
- Cited supporting literature shows: pore canal diameters >300 μm promote bone ingrowth and vascularization in vivo (Karageorgiou & Kaplan, 2005; Abbasi et al., 2020); biomimetic gradient scaffolds with fine internal pillars (~400 μm) and coarse external pillars (~800 μm) promote osseointegration.
- Authors recommend "implants with graded porosity, such as distally increasing axial gradients or inwardly increasing radial gradients" to reduce stress shielding and aseptic loosening (citing Wang et al., 2020).
- "Tissue-engineered scaffolds mimicking the natural bone structure, with a radial gradient of increasing porosity towards the center, have shown enhanced osteogenesis and vascularization."

## Supports / contradicts

- **Strongly supports the biomimetic rationale** of the hypothesis (radial cortical→cancellous gradient design) but **calibrates the magnitude differently**: native rabbit canal diameters are 40–200 μm, not 100–500 μm. The proposed 100→500 μm range is at the upper end of native bone scaling and overlaps with cancellous trabecular spacing rather than canal diameter.
- **Supports** review framings in [[2026-liu-design-and-fabrication-of-biomimetic-gra]], [[2025-liu-gradient-scaffolds-in-bone-soft-tissue-i]], [[2025-xie-recent-advances-in-gradient-biomimetic-s]] that cite this kind of structural benchmark.
- **Contradicts** uniform-pore scaffold designs as physiologically representative.
