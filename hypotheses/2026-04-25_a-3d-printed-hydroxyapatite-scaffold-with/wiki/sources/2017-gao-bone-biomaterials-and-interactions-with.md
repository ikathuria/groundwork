# Gao, Peng, Feng & Shuai (2017) — Bone biomaterials and interactions with stem cells

## Summary

Bone Research review of bone biomaterials with explicit emphasis on stem-cell interaction and **multi-scale (nano/submicro/micro) pore architecture**. Most relevant to the hypothesis: directly cites a study (Nedjari et al.) producing **gradient pore-diameter scaffolds spanning 80 to 360 μm** that "was beneficial for the regeneration of various bone tissues" — published evidence that gradient pore architectures are bioactive in BTE. Also surveys the AM techniques (SLS, SLA, FDM, SLM) and the cortical/cancellous mechanical mismatch problem.

## Key claims

- **Multi-scale pore architecture (nano/submicro/micro) is essential** for biomimicry of the bone ECM microenvironment.
- **Gradient pore-diameter scaffolds (80–360 μm) produced via honeycomb-pattern collectors** "were beneficial for the regeneration of various bone tissues" (Nedjari et al., cited as reference 325) — direct precedent for gradient pore designs.
- Cortical bone is dense (porosity 5–10%) and contains 99% body Ca and 90% body phosphate.
- Bioceramics are "brittle materials with a low fracture toughness, insufficient mechanical strength, and a high elastic modulus compared with those of cortical bone, while polymers are far below the requirements" — modulus mismatch in both directions, motivating composite + graded designs.
- HA [Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂] and TCP [Ca₃(PO₄)₂] have compositions similar to natural bone; they are the "most representative bioactive ceramics."
- HA degradation products provide Ca and P that induce bone regeneration.
- AM techniques surveyed include **SLS, SLA, FDM, SLM** (selective laser melting for metallic scaffolds).
- Pore diameter, shape, interconnectivity, and porosity together determine cell/tissue interaction, mechanical properties, AND degradation behaviour.

## Supports / contradicts

- **Directly supports** [[../hypothesis|the gradient-pore hypothesis]] by citing Nedjari et al.'s 80–360 μm gradient scaffold as beneficial for bone regeneration.
- **Consistent with** the multi-scale porosity argument in [[2012-bose-recent-advances-in-bone-tissue-engineeri]] (macro >100 μm + micro <20 μm).

## Notable quotes

> "Nedjari et al prepared nanofibrous biomaterials with a gradient pore diameter ranging from 80 to 360 μm, depending on the honeycomb pattern size of the collector, which was beneficial for the regeneration of various bone tissues."

> "in order to mimic the ECM microenvironment, bone biomaterials should have a multi-scale porous structure, and the pore diameter, shape, interconnectivity, and porosity not only determine the interactions between bone biomaterials and cells/tissue but also have important effects on the mechanical properties and degradation behavior."

> "bioceramics are brittle materials with a low fracture toughness, insufficient mechanical strength, and a high elastic modulus compared with those of cortical bone"
