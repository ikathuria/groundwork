# Bose, Roy & Bandyopadhyay (2012) — Recent advances in bone tissue engineering scaffolds

## Summary

Trends in Biotechnology review of bone-scaffold materials and processing, with a strong ceramics + additive-manufacturing emphasis. Most directly relevant to the hypothesis: explicitly discusses **graded porosity from center to periphery** as a strategy to balance mechanical integrity with interconnectivity — the conceptual blueprint of a radial pore-size gradient scaffold. Synthesises the consensus pore-size window (200–350 μm) and the macro/micro porosity dual-scale requirement.

## Key claims

- **Macroporosity (>100 μm) AND microporosity (<20 μm) are both required** in an ideal bone scaffold; macropores admit cells/vasculature, micropores increase surface area and protein adsorption.
- **Pore size ≥100 μm** is the minimum for diffusion of nutrients and oxygen; **200–350 μm is the optimum** for bone tissue in-growth (review's own synthesis, with one cited primary).
- A **gradient of porosity from the center to the periphery** of a scaffold (mechanically dense outer shell, more porous core) "can be achieved through complex design and manufacturing that will ensure mechanical integrity and scaffold interconnectivity" — explicit support for the gradient-pore design approach.
- Cancellous bone Young's modulus is **0.1–2 GPa**; cortical bone is **15–20 GPa** — anchors the cortical-to-cancellous gradient that the hypothesis attempts to mimic.
- Microwave-sintered 3D-printed β-TCP scaffolds with **>60% porosity** support osteoblast activity and new bone formation in pores.
- Bioglass scaffolds (70% porous, 300–400 μm pores) form an HCA layer that significantly enhances osteoblast activity in vitro.
- BCP scaffolds (80 ± 3% HA / 20 ± 3% β-TCP) with 70% interconnected porosity (68% pores at 400 μm) support new bone formation in immunodeficient mice.
- Increasing porosity reduces compressive strength and increases manufacturing complexity — the tradeoff that motivates gradient designs.

## Supports / contradicts

- **Strongly supports** [[../hypothesis|the gradient-pore HA hypothesis]] by explicitly proposing center-to-periphery porosity gradients as a design strategy.
- **Consistent with** [[2012-amini-bone-tissue-engineering-recent-advances]] on the 100–300 μm window and with [[2014-polo-corrales-scaffold-design-for-bone-regeneration]] on the porosity/strength tradeoff.
- **Slightly narrower optimum** (200–350 μm) than [[2016-murphy-scaffolds-and-cells-for-tissue-regeneration]] (300–500 μm for mineralization).
