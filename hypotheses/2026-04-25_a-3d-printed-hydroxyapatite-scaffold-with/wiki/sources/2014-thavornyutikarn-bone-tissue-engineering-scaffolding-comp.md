# Thavornyutikarn et al. (2014) — Bone tissue engineering scaffolding: computer-aided scaffolding techniques

## Summary

Comprehensive review of computer-aided / additive-manufacturing approaches to bone scaffolds, covering CAD-driven design, the major rapid-prototyping techniques (FDM, SLS, SLA, 3DP/binder jet, robocasting, two-photon polymerization), and their material/architecture combinations. Most relevant to the hypothesis: explicitly highlights **functionally graded scaffolds** that mimic the anatomical geometry of bone produced via SLS + CAD, and tabulates the modulus gap between common scaffold composites and cancellous bone — the engineering target of the gradient-pore strategy.

## Key claims

- **Compressive modulus of a composite scaffold depends on porosity, pore size, AND ceramic/glass content** — three independent design knobs the hypothesis must control.
- **Few composite scaffolds reach cancellous-bone modulus**; most fall below — direct mechanical motivation for graded architectures.
- "Functional gradient scaffolds that mimic the anatomical geometry of bone have also been produced from PCL using the SLS technique combined with CAD design" — graded-architecture scaffolds are an established CAD/AM output.
- An ideal TE scaffold is biocompatible, biodegradable, **highly porous AND interconnected**, and mechanically reliable.
- **PCL maintains osteoblast phenotype** as a long-term implant in BTE.
- Cortical-bone fracture toughness is 6–8 MPa·m^½, vs ~1 MPa·m^½ for HA and ~0.5–1 MPa·m^½ for 45S5 Bioglass — illustrates ceramic brittleness vs. bone.
- Bone matrix is ~70 wt% carbonated HA in a collagen/water matrix.
- Tabulates degradation timelines: PDLLA 12–16 months, PLLA >24 months, PGA 6–12 months, PCL >24 months — relevant for resorbable composites if HA is matrixed with polymer.
