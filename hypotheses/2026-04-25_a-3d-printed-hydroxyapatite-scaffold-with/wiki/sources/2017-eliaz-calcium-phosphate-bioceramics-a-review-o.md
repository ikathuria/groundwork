# Eliaz & Metoki 2017 — Calcium Phosphate Bioceramics review

## Summary

A comprehensive review (Materials, 2017) of calcium-phosphate (CaP) bioceramics covering history, crystal structure, mechanical/chemical properties, coating technologies (with focus on electrochemical processes), and biomedical applications across orthopaedic and dental indications. Notably introduces and contextualises **functionally graded materials (FGMs)** — materials with chemical or structural gradients designed to combine surface biocompatibility with bulk mechanical strength — directly aligned with the rationale for our radial pore-size gradient. Also documents native cortical/cancellous bone properties (porosity ~6% vs. ~80%; trabecular thickness 50–300 μm; cancellous E = 50–500 MPa) used as design targets.

## Key claims

- **Native bone targets**: cortical bone has ~6% porosity; cancellous bone has ~80% porosity with **trabeculae 50–300 μm thick**; cancellous Young's modulus 50–500 MPa, tensile strength 1.2–20 MPa, K_IC = 0.1 MPa·m^(1/2). These are the benchmarks our gradient HA scaffold must straddle.
- **Functionally graded materials (FGMs)** "exhibit either chemical composition gradient or structural gradient … allowing properties that cannot be achieved otherwise, for example exhibiting good biocompatibility — a surface governed property, with good mechanical strength — a bulk governed property." This is the design principle behind a pore-size gradient.
- CaP family covered: stoichiometric HA (Ca/P = 1.67), calcium-deficient HA (CDHA), α-/β-TCP, biphasic CaP (BCP), with phase-purity, dissolution-rate, and processing trade-offs documented.
- Limitations of bulk CaP scaffolds noted: degradation rates often un-matched with new-bone growth, "improper pore size and structure for bone ingrowth," and poor mechanical strength — motivating the gradient architecture.
- Reviews characterization toolkit: XRD phase analysis, SEM, EDX, BET surface area, TGA, ICP-MS, FDA/ISO standards for CaP coatings.
