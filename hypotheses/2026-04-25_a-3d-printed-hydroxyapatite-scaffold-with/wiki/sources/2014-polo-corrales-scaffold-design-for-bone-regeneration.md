# Polo-Corrales, Latorre-Esteves & Ramirez-Vick (2014) — Scaffold Design for Bone Regeneration

## Summary

Comprehensive scaffold-design review (J Nanosci Nanotechnol) covering biological context (bone biology, osteoblast/osteoclast biology, BMPs), material classes, and architectural parameters. Cites the canonical Karageorgiou & Kaplan 2005 review (reference 252 in this paper) for the porosity-vs-strength tradeoff, and the Hulbert 1970 canine-pore-size series (reference 29) for the **15–50 μm fibrovascular / 50–150 μm osteoid / 150–500 μm mineralized** ingrowth gradient — directly relevant to the gradient-pore mechanism of the hypothesis. Also reports an MC3T3 study showing osteoblast adhesion correlates with a **mineralization gradient** within a scaffold.

## Key claims

- **Pore size and interconnectivity directly determine scaffold porosity**, and porosity in turn modulates osteogenesis: "osteogenic cells have been shown to respond to particular pore dimensions."
- **In canines, pore-size determines the type of tissue ingrowth**: 15–50 μm → fibrovascular tissue; 50–150 μm → osteoid; **150–500 μm → mineralized bone** (citing Hulbert et al. 1970, reference 29). This is the empirical base under the hypothesis's pore-size gradient.
- **Increased porosity / pore size enhances bone ingrowth but reduces mechanical strength**, compromising structural integrity (cited to Karageorgiou & Kaplan 2005, reference 252 — the canonical review of scaffold porosity for bone TE).
- A **mineralization gradient** in calcium-phosphate-loaded gelatin scaffolds biases MC3T3 osteoblast adhesion/proliferation toward higher-mineral regions — a graded-property scaffold drives spatial cell behavior.
- Hierarchical structure of bone spans **>3 mm (solid) → 75–200 μm (trabeculae) → 1–20 μm (lamellae) → 60–600 nm (collagen-mineral)** and 10–500 μm (osteons). The cortical/cancellous transition the hypothesis targets is one slice of this hierarchy.
- Cortical bone has lower turnover than trabecular but provides primary biomechanical strength.
- Pre-osteoblasts respond to BMP-2/BMP-7, TGF-β, FGF, IGF, PDGF, and interleukins to migrate, proliferate and differentiate.
- Coral-derived HA scaffolds with 600 μm average pore size support controlled bone formation in non-human primates (Papio ursinus) — large-pore HA is biologically active in vivo.

## Supports / contradicts

- **Strongly supports** [[../hypothesis|the gradient-pore hypothesis]] by providing the canine pore-size-vs-tissue-type empirical scaffold and the mineralization-gradient → cell-response data.
- **Cites** Karageorgiou & Kaplan (2005) (Biomaterials 26:5474) — the standard reference for the **>100 μm minimum, ~300 μm optimal pore size** synthesis. (Note: this paper itself does not directly quote those numbers from K&K but cites it for the porosity/strength tradeoff.)
- **Consistent with** [[2012-amini-bone-tissue-engineering-recent-advances]], [[2012-bose-recent-advances-in-bone-tissue-engineeri]], [[2016-murphy-scaffolds-and-cells-for-tissue-regeneration]] on the basic porosity/pore-size requirements.

## Notable quotes

> "bone growth in canines where the pore diameters range 15–50 mm is fibrovascular, 50–150 mm is osteoid, and 150–500 mm is mineralized." (units in original are mistyped as "mm"; from Hulbert et al. data they are μm.)

> "An increase in these properties [porosity and pore size] helps bone ingrowth but reduce the mechanical strength compromising their structural integrity." (citing Karageorgiou & Kaplan 2005)

> "MC3T3 cells adhered to regions with higher calcium phosphate content along the scaffold, which indicate that the mineralization gradient affects the adhesion/proliferation of cells and physical properties of the scaffold."
