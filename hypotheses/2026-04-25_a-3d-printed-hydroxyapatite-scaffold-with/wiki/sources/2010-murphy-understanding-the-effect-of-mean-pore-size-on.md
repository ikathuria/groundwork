## Summary

Murphy and O'Brien commentary synthesises their group's prior work using freeze-dried collagen-glycosaminoglycan (CG) scaffolds spanning a wide range of mean pore sizes (85–325 μm) seeded with MC3T3-E1 osteoblasts. They show a non-linear relationship between pore size and cell number over 7 days: scaffolds with the largest pores (325 μm) had the highest cell number at all time points despite lower specific surface area, while the smallest pores (96–120 μm) showed only an early transient peak (24–48 h) in cell attachment before being overtaken. This is a key foundational paper for resolving the literature contradiction between "small pores are better for attachment" (96–150 μm optimal) and "large pores are better for ingrowth" (300–800 μm optimal). It directly supports the proposed mechanism in the gradient-pore hypothesis: small surface pores favor early attachment via ligand density; large interior pores favor later proliferation/migration and avoid edge-effect cell aggregation that limits diffusion.

## Key claims

- CG scaffolds produced by lyophilisation with controlled freezing (−40 to −10 °C final) span pore sizes 96–151 μm; novel annealing step extends range to **85–325 μm** (Haugh, Murphy, O'Brien 2009).
- Specific surface area for tetrakaidecahedral cellular solid: SA/V = 0.718 / d (where d = mean pore diameter); thus SA scales inversely with pore size.
- **Non-linear pore-size effect on cell number over 7 days** in MC3T3-E1: large 325 μm pores supported highest cell number at all time points; 120 μm pores showed transient peak at 24–48 h (consistent with ligand-density-mediated initial adhesion) that disappeared by day 7.
- Cell aggregation forming a "skin" around scaffold edges in small-pore (85–120 μm) scaffolds restricts diffusion of nutrients to interior cells and suppresses interior colonization.
- As pore size increases, cells migrate further into the scaffold; at 325 μm cells colonize the centre.
- Earlier 48 h study by O'Brien et al. (2005) showed *opposite* finding: 96 μm gave highest attachment — but only at 48 h, on a narrower pore range (96–151 μm). Both findings are reconciled when the wider range (85–325 μm) is studied for ≥7 days.
- Authors hypothesise optimal pore size varies by cell type; demonstrated for MC3T3-E1 pre-osteoblasts in this study.
- Literature context: 75–100 μm minimum for bone growth (Hulbert 1970); >300 μm needed for vascularization and bone ingrowth; <300 μm encourages osteochondral ossification (Kuboki 2001; Tsuruga 1997).
- Mechanical caveat: as pore size increases further, void volume compromises scaffold mechanical properties; eventually surface area drops below cell-adhesion threshold.

## Supports / contradicts

- **Resolves contradiction** between O'Brien et al. 2005 (96 μm optimal at 48 h) and large-pore studies (300+ μm optimal for bone ingrowth) — both correct at their respective time points and pore ranges.
- **Foundational support** for [[2024-diez-escudero-in-vitro-evaluation-of-pore-size-graded-bone]] and [[2025-conoscenti-continuous-pore-size-gradient-enhances-z]] gradient designs that pair small surface pores (attachment) with large interior pores (proliferation/migration).
- **Directly supports the hypothesis under test**: pairing small-pore outer (attachment, surface area) with large-pore core (proliferation, diffusion) is exactly what their data implies as optimal.
