# Henkel et al. (2013) — Bone Regeneration Based on Tissue Engineering Conceptions

## Summary

Bone Research review tracing the conceptual evolution of bone tissue engineering and the requirements for the "tissue engineered construct" (TEC). Important for the hypothesis: explicitly endorses **scaffolds with gradients in porosity and pore size** as a strategy for reproducing multi-tissue interfaces and directing osteogenesis vs. osteochondral ossification spatially. Provides the multi-scale hierarchical-structure baseline (cortical/cancellous, osteons, lamellae, fibrillar collagen, mineral) the gradient hypothesis is meant to mimic.

## Key claims

- "Adequate porosity and pore size is crucial" for scaffolds to support vascularisation and body-fluid supply throughout the TEC.
- **Trabecular (cancellous) bone porosity is 50–90%** with average trabecular spacing ~1 mm and density ~0.2 g·cm⁻³ — sets the geometric target for the cancellous-mimicking core of a gradient scaffold.
- **Scaffolds with gradients in porosity and pore size** can be functionalised to support vascularisation and direct osteogenesis in one zone while promoting osteochondral ossification in another — i.e., gradient pore architecture is a known multi-tissue-interface design pattern.
- Bone is a hierarchical composite: **macro (cortical/cancellous), micro (Haversian, osteons, trabeculae), sub-micro (lamellae), nano (collagen + mineral)**. Replicating cortical-to-cancellous transition is one element of biomimetic scaffold design.
- Mineral hydroxyapatite has Young's modulus ~130 GPa and tensile strength ~100 MPa; collagen Young's modulus is 1–2 GPa, tensile strength 50–1000 MPa — the composite properties of bone arise from this combination.
- HA + tricalcium phosphate combinations as bone-substitute material in the Masquelet (induced membrane) technique have so far yielded inferior results to autologous bone graft — a clinical translation caveat.

## Supports / contradicts

- **Supports** the [[../hypothesis|gradient-pore hypothesis]] conceptually by endorsing graded porosity / pore size as a way to reproduce tissue-interface biology.
- **Consistent with** [[2012-amini-bone-tissue-engineering-recent-advances]] and [[2012-bose-recent-advances-in-bone-tissue-engineeri]] on the cortical/cancellous mechanical-property gradient.
- Less specific on absolute pore-size optima than Bose 2012 or Murphy/Bruzauskaite 2016 — operates at higher conceptual level.

## Notable quotes

> "scaffolds with gradients in porosity and pore sizes can be functionalised to allow vascularisation and direct osteogenesis in one area of the scaffold, while promoting osteochondral ossification in the other, which is an appealing approach to reproduce multiple tissues and tissue interfaces within one and the same biomaterial scaffold."

> "trabecular bone has a porosity of 50–90% with an average trabecular spacing of around 1mm and an average density of approximately 0.2 g·cm⁻³"

> "Cancellous bone consists of interconnecting framework of rod and plate shaped trabeculae."
