# Tang et al. 2025 — Advances in 3D-Printed Scaffolds for Bone Defect Repair

## Summary

Recent (late-2025) systematic review of 3D-printed scaffolds for bone defect repair, comparing natural / synthetic polymers, ceramics, and composites, and explicitly endorsing **gradient and multi-material ceramic scaffolds** as the leading-edge strategy for matching native bone's hierarchical and site-specific demands. Provides a method-comparison chart (FDM, DIW, SLA/DLP, SLS, LDM, bioprinting) with pros/cons including resolution, material range, and cytotoxicity considerations. Strong direct support for the gradient-pore HA hypothesis.

## Key claims

- "**In parallel, gradient and multi-material ceramic scaffolds enable spatial control of composition, stiffness, and degradation, better matching the hierarchical and site-specific demands of native bone.**"
- 3D printing now enables defect-matched, patient-specific architectures with prescribed pore size, interconnectivity, and anisotropy.
- β-TCP is more readily resorbed and clinically favoured over α-TCP; both benefit from pore sizes in the hundred-micrometer range to balance transport and stability.
- HA / polymer composites fabricated by SLS show improved compressive strength and osseointegration vs polymer-only scaffolds.
- DLP projects entire layer images for **higher throughput than scanning SLA**; both give voxel-level control.
- Light-based printing (SLA, DLP) provides high resolution and complex microarchitectures including vascular-like channels but **material options are limited and photoinitiator cytotoxicity must be considered**.
- Low-temperature deposition manufacturing (LDM) yields **hierarchical porosity from micro- to nanoscale** by combining printing with phase separation and avoids thermal degradation of bioactivity — a candidate route alternative to DLP/sintering.
- Hierarchical pore networks provide multi-scale channels facilitating nutrient diffusion, ion exchange, and vascular ingrowth; this collectively promotes osteogenesis and accelerated defect bridging.
- Inadequate solvent exchange or non-ideal freezing trajectories in LDM lead to residual solvent, shrinkage, or pore collapse; mitigation via solvent ratios, cooling rates, secondary crosslinking, post-infiltration.
- Magnesium and silver doping enhance osteogenesis / antibacterial performance but Ag dosage must be balanced against cytotoxicity.

## Supports / contradicts

- Strongest direct support among the batch for the hypothesis: gradient ceramic scaffolds are explicitly endorsed as the leading strategy for biomimetic load-bearing constructs.
- Reinforces the AM-route trade-offs: DLP provides resolution but adds photoinitiator-cytotoxicity risk; LDM provides hierarchical porosity but adds solvent-residue / shrinkage risk. Both must be tested against the gradient-pore HA design.
- Aligns with [[2023-koushik-bone-tissue-engineering-scaffolds-functi]], [[2025-hossen-biomimetic-strategies-for-bone-regenerat]] on hierarchical / multi-material gradient design.
